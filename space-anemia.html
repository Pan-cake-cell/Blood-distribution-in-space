Space Anemia: What Happens to Blood in Space?

When astronauts spend time in space, their bodies start to destroy red blood cells faster than normal. As a result, they can develop a condition called space anaemia.

What happens in space?
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Answer
🌍 On Earth: the body destroys about 2 million red blood cells per second. 🌌 In space: this increases to ~3 million per second (54% more). ⇒ After long missions, almost half of astronauts are anemic.

Why astronauts don't feel it in space
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Answer
In weightlessness, the body doesn't have to work against gravity. Because of this, having fewer red blood cells doesn't cause major problems while astronauts are in orbit.

04
What happens after landing?
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Answer
Back on Earth, the body must work against gravity again. With fewer red blood cells delivering oxygen, astronauts may feel tired, weak, and less physically strong. The good news: space anaemia is temporary. Blood levels return to normal within 3–4 months. Scientists think the body may use fat stored in bone marrow as energy to help produce new red blood cells faster.

Why this matters?
