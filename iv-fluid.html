IV Fluid in Space

Module Introduction

IV fluid — Intravenous (IV) fluids are sterile liquids—commonly containing water, electrolytes (salt), and glucose (sugar)—delivered directly into a vein to restore hydration, maintain electrolyte balance, or administer medication.

Why astronauts need IV fluid in space
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Detail
Astronauts require intravenous (IV) fluids in space primarily to combat chronic dehydration caused by microgravity-induced fluid shifts, which cause kidneys to increase urine output. This fluid loss, along with potential space motion sickness, necessitates medical hydration, particularly for long-duration missions and re-entry preparation.

Absence of gravity
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Detail
In microgravity, fluids do not flow normally because there's no gravity to help push them through tubes. Instead, liquids form floating blobs and trap air bubbles, which can be dangerous because those bubbles could enter the bloodstream and could potentially be life-threatening.

Fluid and Electrolyte Considerations
▶ TAP TO REVEAL
◀ Detail
In reduced-gravity environments, the body regulates fluids differently. Fluid shifts and changes in kidney function can disrupt the normal balance of water and electrolytes. For example, on Mars, people may gradually lose plasma volume, which would change their fluid and electrolyte requirements over time. Because of this, IV fluid therapy would need to be carefully adjusted rather than following standard Earth-based protocols. Close monitoring of blood electrolyte levels and urine output would be essential to prevent complications such as high potassium levels, low sodium levels, or dehydration.
